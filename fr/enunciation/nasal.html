FRENCH NASAL VOWELS

A Guide to Nasal Vowel Pronunciation.

** Click on the down arrows to see the texts below:

👃

French has 4 nasal vowel sounds (no equivalent in English)

  • Air flows through both mouth AND nose simultaneously
Nasal vowels occur when vowel + n/m are in the same syllable
  • an, en, in, on, un → nasal sounds
The n/m is silent when creating a nasal vowel
  • Don't pronounce the "n" - let air flow through your nose instead

/ɑ̃/ - Nasal A sound

  • an: dans, blanc, France
  • am (before b, p): chambre, lampe
  • en: vent, enfant
  • em (before b, p): membre, exemple
  • aon (rare): paon, faon

How to pronounce:

  • Say /a/ as in "father"
  • Lower your soft palate to let air flow through nose
  • Don't pronounce the "n" - it nasalizes the vowel

/ɔ̃/ - Nasal O sound

(The French nasal vowel /ɔ̃/ is similar to the vowel in British "born", but without the /r/ sound and with nasal resonance.)

  • on: bon, mon, maison
  • om (before b, p): tomber, pompier

How to pronounce:

  • Say /ɔ/ (open O) as in "caught"
  • Round your lips
  • Let air flow through nose
  • Don't pronounce the "n"

Common words:

  • bonjour, non, pont, long, onze

/ɛ̃/ - Nasal I/E sound

  • in: vin, matin
  • im (before b, p): impossible, simple
  • ain: pain, main, demain
  • aim: faim
  • ein: plein, frein
  • yn, ym: synthèse, symbole

How to pronounce:

  • Say /ɛ/ (open E) as in "bed"
  • Let air flow through nose
  • Spread lips slightly (NOT rounded)

/œ̃/ - Nasal EU sound (disappearing in modern French!)

  • un: un, brun, lundi
  • um: parfum, humble

Important note:

  • Most French speakers now pronounce this as /ɛ̃/ (same as IN)
  • Only some older speakers and certain regions maintain the distinction
  • un and brin sound the same for most people

How to pronounce (traditional):

  • Say /œ/ (open EU) with rounded lips
  • Let air flow through nose

Vowel + n/m + VOWEL = NOT nasal! (except with prefixes)

  • une /yn/ - oral /y/ + pronounced /n/
  • année /ane/ - oral /a/ + pronounced /n/
  • bonne /bɔn/ - oral /ɔ/ + pronounced /n/
  • inutile /inytil/ - oral /i/ + /n/

Compare nasal vs oral:

  • bon /bɔ̃/ (nasal) vs bonne /bɔn/ (oral + n)
  • an /ɑ̃/ (nasal) vs Anne /an/ (oral + n)
  • vin /vɛ̃/ (nasal) vs vine /vin/ (oral + n)

Double n/m:

  • Always denasalized: année, bonne, femme, immense
  • **Exception: nasalize if the double n or m results from a prefix, (en/em/im- + root): emmener, ennuyer, immangeable
    • prefix: en- / em- (put into, cause to be)
    • prefix: in- / im- / il- / ir- (not)

M replaces N before B and P:

  • chambre, lampe (not "chanbre" or "lanpe")
  • impossible, timbre
  • nombre, pompe

Exception: bonbon

  • Uses N before B (compound word: bon + bon)

Quick reference:

  • /ɑ̃/: an, am, en, em
  • /ɔ̃/: on, om
  • /ɛ̃/: in, im, ain, aim, ein, yn, ym
  • /œ̃/: un, um (rare/disappearing)

Common mistakes to avoid:

  • ❌ Don't pronounce the "n" or "m" - it's silent!
  • ❌ Don't add an extra syllable
  • ❌ Don't make it sound like English "ng"

Test yourself:

  • Hold your nose while speaking. If it's truly nasal, you'll feel the difference!
  • Air should flow through both mouth and nose

Practice sequence:

  • 1. Say oral vowel: /a/, /ɔ/, /ɛ/
  • 2. Keep saying it and lower soft palate
  • 3. Feel air coming through nose → nasal!

Words that look nasal but aren't:

  • examen /ɛgzamɛn/ - ends with oral /ɛ/ + /n/
  • abdomen /abdɔmɛn/ - same pattern
  • amen /amɛn/ - religious context

Feminine forms denasalize:

  • bon /bɔ̃/ → bonne /bɔn/
  • un /œ̃/ → une /yn/
  • brun /brœ̃/ → brune /bryn/

Foreign words:

  • Latin/English borrowed words may keep oral pronunciation
  • in vitro, in situ - often oral /in/

Nasal vowel + liaison = nasal vowel + pronounced n

  • un ami → /œ̃n‿ami/ - nasal + pronounced /n/
  • bon ami → /bɔ̃n‿ami/ - nasal + liaison /n/
  • mon enfant → /mɔ̃n‿ɑ̃fɑ̃/ - the /n/ is pronounced

Compare:

  • bon /bɔ̃/ (isolated) - nasal
  • bon garçon /bɔ̃ gaʀsɔ̃/ (no liaison) - stays nasal
  • bon ami /bɔ̃n‿ami/ (liaison) - /n/ pronounced

The nasal vowel remains, but n/m is pronounced in liaison

PRO-TIPS FOR MASTERING NASAL VOWELS

Nasal vowels are one of the most distinctive features of French:

  • Physical sensation:
    • You should feel vibration in your nasal cavity. Hold your nose gently while speaking to feel it!
  • Most common error:
    • English speakers tend to pronounce the "n" or "m". Remember: these letters nasalize the vowel, then disappear!
  • The 4-to-3 reduction:
    • Modern French essentially has 3 nasal vowels since /œ̃/ (UN) merged with /ɛ̃/ (IN) for most speakers.
  • Gender reveals nasality:
    • Many masculine adjectives are nasal, but feminine forms are oral: bon/bonne, brun/brune, plein/pleine.
  • Practice with minimal pairs:
    • The best way to train your ear and mouth is through contrasting pairs (see below).
🎯 MINIMAL PAIRS - MASTER THE DISTINCTIONS
  • Nasal vs Oral: bon /bɔ̃/ vs bonne /bɔn/, an /ɑ̃/ vs Anne /an/, vin /vɛ̃/ vs vine /vin/
  • /ɑ̃/ vs /ɔ̃/: banc (bench) vs bon (good), temps (time) vs ton (your), cent (hundred) vs son (his)
  • /ɑ̃/ vs /ɛ̃/: vent (wind) vs vin (wine), blanc (white) vs brin (strand), sans (without) vs sain (healthy)
  • /ɔ̃/ vs /ɛ̃/: pont (bridge) vs pain (bread), long (long) vs lin (linen), son (his) vs sain (healthy)
  • Denasalization: bon /bɔ̃/ vs bonne /bɔn/, fin /fɛ̃/ vs fine /fin/, an /ɑ̃/ vs année /ane/
