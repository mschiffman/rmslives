FRENCH LIAISON RULES

A Guide to Linking Words in French.

** Click on the down arrows to see the texts below:

🔗

Liaison = linking a silent final consonant to a following vowel

  • les amis → /lez‿ami/ (the silent "s" is pronounced as /z/)
Three types: Obligatory, Optional, and Forbidden
  • Obligatory: you MUST make liaison (formal and informal speech)
  • Optional: you CAN make liaison (depends on register and rhythm)
  • Forbidden: you must NEVER make liaison
Common liaison consonants
  • s, x, z → pronounced /z/: les‿amis, deux‿ans, chez‿elle
  • t, d → pronounced /t/: petit‿ami, grand‿homme
  • n → pronounced /n/: un‿ami, mon‿ami
  • r → pronounced /r/: premier‿étage
Liaison creates a smooth link and avoids a hard break before a vowel
  • Creates smooth, flowing French speech

1. Determiner + Noun/Adjective

  • les‿amis, des‿enfants, mes‿amis, ces‿années
  • un‿homme, deux‿ans, trois‿heures
  • quel‿âge, quelques‿uns, plusieurs‿autres

2. Adjective + Noun (when before noun)

  • petit‿ami, grand‿homme, bon‿appétit
  • un gros‿ours, les beaux‿arts

3. Pronoun + Verb / Verb + Pronoun

  • nous‿avons, ils‿ont, on‿a
  • sont‿ils, peut‿on, va-t‿il

4. One-syllable preposition/adverb + word

  • en‿été, dans‿un, chez‿elle, sous‿un
  • très‿important, plus‿ou moins, bien‿sûr

1. After singular noun

  • un étudiant | américain (NOT ‿américain)
  • le garçon | est (NOT ‿est)

2. After "et" (and)

  • lui et | elle (NOT et‿elle)
  • Paul et | Anne (NOT et‿Anne)

3. Before "h aspiré"

  • les | héros (NOT lez‿héros)
  • des | haricots (NOT dez‿haricots)
  • en | haut (NOT en‿haut)

4. After a pause

  • Comment allez-vous | aujourd'hui? (pause blocks liaison)

5. Special case (common in speech):

  • les | onze (often no liaison)

1. After plural noun

  • les enfants‿adorent OR les enfants | adorent

2. After verb (except être/avoir)

  • je vais‿aller OR je vais | aller
  • ils parlent‿anglais OR ils parlent | anglais

3. After multi-syllable prepositions/adverbs

  • pendant‿un mois OR pendant | un mois
  • toujours‿aussi OR toujours | aussi

4. After "pas"

  • pas‿encore OR pas | encore

More common in:

  • Formal speech, reading, singing
  • Fixed expressions

S, X, Z → /z/ sound

  • les‿amis /lez‿ami/
  • deux‿ans /døz‿ɑ̃/
  • chez‿elle /ʃez‿ɛl/

T, D → /t/ sound (d becomes voiceless!)

  • petit‿ami /pətit‿ami/
  • grand‿homme /gʀɑ̃t‿ɔm/ (NOT /d/)

N → /n/ sound (nasal vowel + /n/)

  • un‿ami /œ̃n‿ami/ (nasal + /n/)
  • bon‿appétit /bɔ̃n‿apeti/

R → /r/ sound

  • premier‿étage /pʀəmjer‿etaʒ/

H aspiré prevents liaison (and elision)

  • les | héros ❌ NOT lez‿héros
  • des | haricots ❌ NOT dez‿haricots
  • le héros ❌ NOT l'héros

Common h aspiré words:

  • 🧍‍♂️ héros, handicapé, Hollandais
  • 🏠 haricot, haut, hors, honte
  • 🌿 hibou, houx

H muet ALLOWS liaison:

  • les‿hommes, des‿histoires, l'‿hôtel

Must be memorized - dictionaries mark it!

Always make liaison in these common phrases:

  • comment‿allez-vous
  • c'est-à-dire
  • de temps‿en temps
  • tout‿à fait
  • plus‿ou moins
  • États-Unis /etaz‿yni/
  • Champs-Élysées /ʃɑ̃z‿elize/
  • avant‿hier
  • petit‿à petit
  • nuit‿et jour (but no liaison after "et"!)

These are learned as units

Numbers usually require liaison:

  • deux‿ans /døz‿ɑ̃/
  • trois‿heures /tʀwaz‿œʀ/
  • six‿enfants /siz‿ɑ̃fɑ̃/
  • dix‿ans /diz‿ɑ̃/
  • vingt‿ans /vɛ̃t‿ɑ̃/

Special cases - NO liaison:

  • les | onze (onze starts with vowel but blocks liaison)
  • les | huit (huit has h aspiré)

Six and dix change pronunciation:

  • Final position: six /sis/, dix /dis/
  • Before consonant: six chats /si ʃa/, dix chats /di ʃa/
  • Before vowel: six‿ans /siz‿ɑ̃/, dix‿ans /diz‿ɑ̃/

Formal/Careful Speech:

  • More optional liaisons
  • Reading aloud, speeches, news
  • Les enfants‿aiment jouer

Informal/Casual Speech:

  • Fewer optional liaisons
  • Everyday conversation
  • Les enfants | aiment jouer

Careful with optional liaisons:

  • Too many = overly formal, stilted
  • Too few = acceptable in conversation
  • Obligatory liaisons are ALWAYS required!

Regional variation exists:

  • Southern French may have fewer liaisons

1. Making forbidden liaisons:

  • ❌ et‿elle → ✅ et | elle
  • ❌ lez‿héros → ✅ les | héros

2. Wrong pronunciation:

  • ❌ grand‿homme /gʀɑ̃d‿ɔm/ → ✅ /gʀɑ̃t‿ɔm/ (t not d!)

3. Forgetting obligatory liaisons:

  • ❌ les | amis → ✅ lez‿amis
  • ❌ ils | ont → ✅ ilz‿ont

4. Over-doing optional liaisons:

  • Sounds unnatural in casual speech

5. Cuir & Velours (errors with names):

  • Cuir: adding /t/ where it doesn't exist: ❌ il a‿t‿un chat
  • Velours: using /z/ instead of /t/: ❌ vingt‿z‿ans
💡
PRO-TIPS FOR MASTERING LIAISON

Liaison is what makes French sound smooth and flowing:

  • Start with obligatory liaisons:
    • Focus on determiner + noun, pronoun + verb first. These are essential and always required.
  • Don't overthink optional liaisons:
    • In casual conversation, it's perfectly fine to skip most optional liaisons. Native speakers do!
  • Remember the big three forbidden contexts:
    • After "et," before "h aspiré," and after singular nouns. These are the most common mistakes.
  • Listen for rhythm, not just rules:
    • Liaison creates smooth flow. If making liaison feels choppy or awkward, it's probably optional or forbidden.
  • Practice fixed expressions as units:
    • Learn common phrases with their liaisons built in (comment allez-vous, États-Unis, etc.)
🎯 QUICK REFERENCE - LIAISON SUMMARY
  • ✅ Always make liaison: determiner + noun/adj, adj + noun (before), pronoun + verb, short prepositions
  • ❌ Never make liaison: after singular noun, after "et", before h aspiré, before onze/huit/un (numbers)
  • 🤔 Optional liaison: after plural noun, after verb (not être/avoir), after long prepositions, after "pas"
  • 📝 Pronunciation: s/x/z → /z/, t/d → /t/, n → /n/, r → /r/
  • 🎭 Context matters: Formal = more liaisons, Informal = fewer optional liaisons
