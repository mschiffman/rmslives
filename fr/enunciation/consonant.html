Many French consonant sounds resemble English ones, but their pronunciation rules differ.

  • t, p, c, k sounds are not aspirated as in English. ()
  • r is a throat sound (uvular), closer to a gargling sound than the English “r”. ()
  • ch is pronounced as "sh" in English. ()
  • ç is pronounced as "s" in English. ()
  • j is pronounced as "s" in "pleasure" in English. ()
  • qu is pronounced as "k" in English. ()
  • th is pronounced as "t" in English. ()
  • gn is pronounced as "ny" in "canyon" in English. ()

PRO-TIPS & EXCEPTIONS

French consonant pronunciation depends more on word structure and grammar than on spelling alone.

  • "H" muet and "H" aspiré:
    • "H" is always silent, but "H" aspiré blocks elision/liaison ( ❌l’héros, ❌lez‿haricots)
  • Double consonants:
    • Usually pronounced as a single sound (e.g., /fam/), except in some cases where -ILL- represents the /j/ (“y”) sound (e.g., /fij/, /famij/). But in words like " /vil/" and " /mil/" -ILL- retains the hard L sound.
  • Final "S" for plural:
    • Always silent except when liaison makes it sound like /z/ (e.g., les amis /lez‿ami/).
